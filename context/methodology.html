OliveTheory — Research Methodology

A computational pipeline from hold music to predicted escape motivation

~50% of 988 Suicide Hotline callers hang up before reaching a counselor. The music they hear during hold was never selected with any neuroscience. This project builds the missing bridge: audio features → predicted brain activation → escape motivation measurement → evidence-based hold music specifications.

April 2026 Praneel Patel

Three problems, one pipeline.

Problem 1: No existing tool connects music to escape-relevant brain activity. There are models that predict brain responses from audio (TRIBE v2, d'Ascoli 2026; Kell 2018), and there are computational models of escape/quit decisions (Pedersen 2021; Muller 2021; Dombrovski 2013). Nobody has connected them. The bridge between "this audio has these acoustic features" and "this audio shifts the brain toward or away from escape" does not exist.

Problem 2: We can't scan people in crisis. Ethical constraints prevent putting suicidal callers in an fMRI scanner. So we need a way to predict brain responses from audio alone and validate those predictions against known physiology of crisis states from published fMRI studies.

Problem 3: "Escape motivation" is not a single construct. The literature fragments into entrapment (Gilbert & Allan 1998), Pavlovian escape bias (Millner 2018), defensive flight (Mobbs 2020), fatigue-driven quitting (Muller 2021), and value-based decision-making (Dombrovski 2013). We need measurable proxies without collapsing distinct mechanisms.

The pipeline.

Three-stage chain. Each stage is independently validated, each uses published methods, and each has a clear falsification criterion. If any stage fails its benchmark, we stop and fix it before going downstream.

Component A: Audio to brain.

We need whole-brain fMRI predictions from raw audio. The escape-relevant regions—insula, dACC, vmPFC, OFC—are distributed across PFC and limbic cortex. A model that only predicts auditory cortex is useless for this.

Why TRIBE v2
The only model with sufficient coverage

Meta's TRIBE v2 (d'Ascoli et al. 2026) is a foundation model trained on 1,000+ hours of brain recordings across 720 subjects, outputting 20,484 cortical vertices. It predicts the full cortical surface from raw audio at vertex-level resolution.

We evaluated every alternative. Kell (2018) predicts auditory cortex only—too narrow. Ciferri et al. (2025) used CLAP embeddings and got inferior frontal gyrus, but not whole-brain. Casey (2017) showed music features predict OFC at 7T, but that was ridge regression on a single dataset. TRIBE v2 is the only option that gives us the whole brain from raw audio at cortical resolution.

Current limitation: The released checkpoint is cortical-only (20,484 vertices). Subcortical regions—nucleus accumbens, amygdala, caudate—require training a new output head. The architecture supports it (training config exists as run_subcortical.py) but no pre-trained subcortical checkpoint was released. The critical escape-relevant regions (vmPFC, dACC, OFC, anterior insula) are all cortical and available now.

Component B: Brain to escape motivation.

Once we have predicted brain activation, we need to read it for escape-relevant signal. The measurement framework has two layers: validation (can we trust the predictions?) and then application (what does the music do?).

The ruler: Muller's fatigue model

Why Muller 2021
Maps directly to cortical regions we can predict

Muller et al. (2021, Nature Communications) formalized recoverable/unrecoverable fatigue in effort-based choice. The key insight for us: fatigue accumulates in specific cortical regions (RCZp, RCZa, MFG, anterior insula) that are all within TRIBE v2's output space. The model's subjective value signals map to ventral striatum and SFG.

This makes Muller the cleanest available "ruler" for escape motivation—the brain regions it identifies are exactly the regions we can predict, and the temporal dynamics it describes (fatigue accumulation → quit decision) are directly testable with time-series predictions.

The measurement: CEMP

The Cortical Escape Motivation Profile (CEMP) is a predicted activation pattern, not a single scalar. Four regions, each contributing a distinct signal:

Anterior Insula
Aversive salience
Tracks how aversive the current experience feels. Higher activation = more distress signal. Maps to Muller's unrecoverable fatigue pathway.
Dorsal ACC (dACC)
Conflict / quit signal
Registers conflict between "stay" and "go." In Muller's model, the rostral cingulate zone posterior (RCZp) is where fatigue accumulates toward a quit threshold.
Ventromedial PFC
Value integration
Integrates expected value of persistence. Dombrovski (2013) showed this signal is blunted in suicide attempters—the brain's "this might be worth it" circuit is quieter.
Orbitofrontal Cortex
Reward expectation
Weighs expected reward against cost of continuing. The part of the circuit that says "a counselor is coming." Casey (2017) showed this region responds to music features at 7T.

Escape-promoting pattern: High insula + high dACC + low vmPFC + low OFC.

Persistence-promoting pattern: Low insula + high vmPFC + low dACC + stable OFC.

Music that shifts the pattern toward persistence is what we want on 988 hold.

Component C: Music to features.

Curate a library of 50–100 candidate hold music clips. Run each through the pipeline: audio → TRIBE v2 → CEMP score. Extract acoustic features from each clip—tempo, consonance, roughness, spectral centroid, harmonic complexity. Regress features against escape scores.

The output is actionable: which acoustic parameters predict lower escape motivation? That becomes the hold music specification.

Why not MusER
Training data too narrow for hold music

MusER (Ji & Yang 2024, AAAI) decomposes music into emotion-relevant features using a VQ-VAE, but it was trained on EMOPIA—1,087 piano pop clips. It won't generalize to ambient/hold music without retraining. Instead, we use acoustic features extracted directly from audio (tempo, spectral centroid, roughness, mode, harmonic change) and correlate with CEMP scores.

MusER becomes relevant later: once we know which features matter, it (or a retrained variant) could help generate music that optimizes those features. Discovery first, generation second.

Validation: why you should believe this works.

A brain encoding model is only useful if its predictions mean something in the regions we care about. We designed three independent benchmarks, each grounded in published computational neuroscience with known right answers. Each is falsifiable—if any fails, we stop and fix it before moving downstream.

2–6×

Real music produces 2–6× stronger differentiation in escape-relevant prefrontal regions (dACC, vmPFC, OFC, anterior insula) compared to synthetic tones. We tested this first—if the model couldn't distinguish audio in these regions, the whole approach is dead. Anterior insula improved from rank 66 to rank 26. OFC held strong at rank 23. The model is viable for escape-region prediction.

That was the prerequisite—a sensitivity check before committing to benchmarks. Here are the three validation gates:

01

Muller — Temporal Dynamics

Does predicted fatigue accumulate the way the literature says it should?
Muller et al. (2021, Nature Communications) showed cingulate/insula signals decay during aversive effort tasks. We fed 5-minute audio sequences into TRIBE v2 and checked temporal trajectories. Aversive audio: all fatigue regions decrease (slopes −0.05 to −0.33), consistent with fatigue accumulation. Calming audio: all fatigue regions increase (slopes +0.28 to +0.71), consistent with fatigue dissipation. Hold music: flat. The temporal dynamics match.
Passed
02

Dombrovski — Attempter Physiology

Do predictions match known brain patterns in suicide attempters?
Dombrovski et al. (2013, JAMA Psychiatry) showed blunted value signals in vmPFC and ventral striatum in suicide attempters. After fine-tuning on depression fMRI data (ds000171), the model should reproduce these known patterns—reduced vmPFC activation, altered striatal response. The cortical components (vmPFC, insula) are testable now. Full test including ventral striatum requires the subcortical extension.
In progress
03

Pedersen — The Decision Model

Can we extract approach-avoidance parameters from predicted brain signals?
Pedersen et al. (2021, PLOS Computational Biology) mapped approach-avoidance decisions to NAcc, pregenual ACC, subthalamic nucleus, and caudate using an Affective Action Control DDM. Three of four regions are subcortical. We need to train a new output head on HCP 7T subcortical data to access them. This is the question we ultimately care about—does this music shift the stay-or-go decision?—but it's gated behind the subcortical extension.
Needs subcortical
Why this ordering
Known answers first, then the question we care about

Muller and Dombrovski have published brain patterns we can compare against—we know the right answer. If our model reproduces known results, we have reason to trust it for the Pedersen measurement, which is harder to benchmark directly (we don't have AAC task fMRI for our stimuli) but is the actual question: does this music shift the approach-avoidance decision?

This is standard instrument validation: test on known quantities first, then apply to the unknown.

Simulating crisis physiology.

TRIBE v2 was trained on healthy adults. The population calling 988 has altered PFC function, heightened insula reactivity, and blunted striatal reward signals (Dombrovski 2013; Malhi 2022). Using healthy-brain predictions as-is means measuring the wrong neural state.

We also cannot scan people in crisis. That's an ethical wall. So we build a constraint relaxation ladder—fine-tuning the model step by step toward crisis physiology using publicly available fMRI datasets, benchmarking at each rung:

How we validate each step
Benchmark at every rung, not just the last

After each fine-tuning step, we rerun Muller temporal dynamics. Two things must hold: (a) escape-region sensitivity is preserved or improved (no catastrophic forgetting), and (b) predictions shift in the direction predicted by the crisis literature (reduced vmPFC, elevated insula). If either fails, we stop.

The Dombrovski benchmark serves double duty: it validates the instrument AND confirms the fine-tuning moved predictions in the expected direction. If depression-tuned predictions show reduced vmPFC (matching Dombrovski) and accelerated cingulate fatigue (matching Muller), the adaptation is working.

The gap between "suicidal ideation" and "active 988 caller" cannot be bridged empirically. We're transparent about that. But the direction of change is well-characterized: more PFC suppression, more insula activation, blunted reward signals. The ladder gets us as close as the published data allows. The benchmarks tell us whether each step actually worked.

The subcortical extension.

The Pedersen benchmark—the one that measures the actual stay-or-go decision—requires subcortical regions: nucleus accumbens, caudate, subthalamic nucleus. TRIBE v2's released checkpoint doesn't predict these.

Staged training plan
Freeze the encoder, train only the output head

We freeze TRIBE v2's pretrained encoder and train a new linear output head on 8,802 subcortical voxels using HCP 7T movie-watching data (176 subjects—clean OOD test since HCP was in TRIBE's test set, not training). Staged with go/no-go gates:

Stage 0 (~30 min): Ridge regression on frozen features. Gate: R > 0 for NAcc/caudate. If the frozen encoder doesn't contain subcortical signal, nothing downstream will work.

Stage 1 (~5 hr): Trained linear head, frozen backbone. Gate: R improves over ridge.

Stage 2 (~16 hr): Optional backbone fine-tuning if Stage 1 plateaus. Gate: subcortical R improves while cortical R is preserved.

Why we can proceed without it: The critical escape-relevant regions for CEMP (insula, dACC, vmPFC, OFC) are all cortical. The subcortical extension unlocks the Pedersen decision model and adds amygdala/NAcc to the measurement, but the cortical pipeline is operational now.

Deliverables.

Not "play calming music." Two concrete outputs:

Deliverable 01

Evidence-based hold music specification

Specific musical parameters—tempo range, consonance level, spectral profile, harmonic complexity—that minimize predicted escape motivation across a library of 50–100 candidate clips. Derived from brain encoding, not surveys.

Deliverable 02

Reusable measurement instrument

Audio in, escape motivation score out. Screen hold music candidates in minutes. No scanner, no participants, no waiting. Reusable for any new candidate clip, any context.

Why not simpler approaches?

"Test music on callers directly."

Massive sample sizes, can't explain why something works, can't generalize to new music, and the ethics review would take years. Our approach provides mechanistic understanding that transfers to any new stimulus.

"Use emotion recognition models."

"Calming" vs. "anxious" is not the same as "reduces escape motivation in the dACC." Emotion labels are subjective, coarse, and don't map to the specific physiological mechanisms that drive quit decisions. We need region-level specificity, not valence-arousal proxies.

"Use a different brain encoding model."

We evaluated all available options. Kell (2018): auditory cortex only. Ciferri (2025): CLAP-based, gets IFG but not whole-brain. Casey (2017): music features predict OFC at 7T, but ridge regression on one dataset. No model targets PFC/emotion regions from music specifically—this gap is exactly what motivates the project.

Every link in the pipeline is falsifiable. If any stage fails its benchmark, we stop and fix it before moving forward. We haven't had to stop yet.

Current status and roadmap.

Phase 1 Validate the Instrument Current
  • Sensitivity test: TRIBE v2 differentiates audio in escape-relevant regions (2–6× with real music)
  • Muller temporal dynamics: predictions show fatigue accumulation/dissipation consistent with published patterns
  • Subcortical probe: ridge regression on HCP 7T — does frozen encoder contain subcortical signal? (~30 min)
  • Subcortical head training (if probe positive): train output head, validate per-ROI R
  • Dombrovski benchmark: vmPFC/insula/striatal patterns across conditions
  • Retest all benchmarks post-subcortical training (catastrophic forgetting check)
Phase 2 Build the Measurement
  • Formalize CEMP scoring function (weighted combination of region activations)
  • Extend CEMP with subcortical components once head is trained
  • Test Pedersen AAC-DDM parameter extraction from predicted subcortical activations
  • Internal validation: does low-escape music differ systematically from high-escape music?
Phase 3 Generate Recommendations
  • Curate candidate hold music library (50–100 clips, diverse styles)
  • Run full pipeline: audio → TRIBE v2 → escape motivation score
  • Extract acoustic features, correlate with escape scores
  • Identify feature ranges that minimize escape motivation
  • Deliverable: evidence-based hold music specification
Phase 4 External Validation (stretch)
  • Fine-tune on depression fMRI data (ds000171) — constraint relaxation ladder
  • Test whether music recommendations transfer across healthy → depressed predictions
